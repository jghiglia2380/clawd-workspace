## Story Summary
Frances shares her memory of visiting the Día de los Muertos market with her abuelita as a child. Vendors sold cempasúchil (marigolds), sugar skulls, candles, and pan de muerto. Her abuelita taught her that some things have value beyond price — they carry love and memory. In the frame story, the friends decide to create something meaningful for their Community Market, and Riley takes the creative lead for the first time.

## Scene-by-Scene Outline

### Scene 1: Afternoon at Frances's Porch
**Setting:** Frances's front porch, late afternoon light
**Characters:** All four friends, Frances
**Action:** The friends visit Frances to share their Community Market progress. They've learned about trading, timing, and teamwork — but something feels missing.
**Key Visual:** Warm autumn light, friends on porch chairs and steps, Frances in her rocker
**Dialogue Seed:** Layla: "We've figured out how the market will work. But... what makes it special? What makes people care?"

### Scene 2: Frances Understands
**Setting:** Same porch
**Characters:** Frances nodding, friends attentive
**Action:** Frances smiles knowingly. "Ah. You've learned the how. Now you need the why." She settles back in her chair.
**Key Visual:** Frances's warm expression, friends leaning in

---

### Scene 3: The Memory Begins
**Setting:** Porch transitioning
**Characters:** Frances with the memory look
**Action:** Frances's eyes go distant. "When I was nine, my abuelita took me to Mexico. To the market before Día de los Muertos..."
**Key Visual:** Soft light change, transition beginning
**Dialogue Seed:** "Close your eyes. Smell the marigolds. Hear the prayers."

---

### Scene 4: Transition to Memory
**Setting:** Shift to Mexican town square
**Characters:** Visual transformation
**Action:** The porch fades. Colors become vivid — orange, purple, gold. We're in a Mexican town square transformed into a market.
**Key Visual:** Swirling transition, explosion of color and life
**Dialogue Seed:** (Narration) "Every year, the town became something magical. A market not for buying and selling — but for remembering."

---

### Scene 5: Young Frances Arrives
**Setting:** Entrance to the Día de los Muertos market
**Characters:** Young Frances (about 9), Abuelita
**Action:** Young Frances holds her grandmother's hand, eyes wide. The market is overwhelming — colors, sounds, the sweet smell of marigolds everywhere.
**Key Visual:** Little girl holding elderly woman's hand, market stretching before them
**Dialogue Seed:** Young Frances: "Abuelita, why is everything so... orange?" Abuelita: "That's the color that guides them home, mija."

---

### Scene 6: The Marigold Vendors
**Setting:** Flower stall section
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita, flower vendor
**Action:** They approach a stall overflowing with cempasúchil (marigolds). Mountains of orange. The vendor greets Abuelita warmly — they know each other.
**Key Visual:** Spectacular marigold display, warm vendor, intimate community moment
**Dialogue Seed:** Vendor: "Doña Elena! Your flowers are waiting." Abuelita: "The same ones as always. For everyone who's watching."

---

### Scene 7: Why Marigolds?
**Setting:** Same stall
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita explaining
**Action:** Young Frances asks why they need so many flowers. Abuelita explains: the bright color and strong scent guide the spirits home. They're not decoration — they're a path.
**Key Visual:** Close-up of marigolds, young Frances's face understanding something profound
**Dialogue Seed:** "We don't buy these because they're pretty. We buy them to guide our loved ones home."

---

### Scene 8: The Sugar Skull Maker
**Setting:** Sugar skull artisan stall
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita, artisan
**Action:** They visit a woman making sugar skulls (calaveras). She decorates each one with a name — the name of someone who has died.
**Key Visual:** Intricate sugar skulls, artist's focused hands, names written in icing
**Dialogue Seed:** Artisan: "Tell me their names. I write them here so they know we remember." Abuelita: "Antonio. Rosa. Miguel."

---

### Scene 9: Not Just Candy
**Setting:** Same stall
**Characters:** Young Frances watching, moved
**Action:** Young Frances realizes these aren't treats — they're tributes. The artisan puts love into every skull. This is craft with purpose.
**Key Visual:** Young Frances touching a skull gently, reverent expression
**Dialogue Seed:** (Narration) "I'd seen candy skulls before. But I'd never understood. Each one was someone's grandmother. Someone's child. Someone loved."

---

### Scene 10: The Candle Maker
**Setting:** Candle stall, soft glow
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita, candle vendor
**Action:** They buy candles — tall, thin ones for the ofrenda. The vendor explains that each flame is a prayer, a light in the darkness.
**Key Visual:** Rows of candles, warm glow, vendor's weathered hands
**Dialogue Seed:** "The candles guide them through the night. Your prayer is the light."

---

### Scene 11: Pan de Muerto
**Setting:** Bakery stall
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita, baker
**Action:** They buy pan de muerto — special bread shaped with "bones" on top. The baker gives young Frances a small piece to taste. Sweet and sacred.
**Key Visual:** Round loaves with crossed bone shapes, dusted with sugar, warm and fresh
**Dialogue Seed:** Baker: "We bake with our hands what we carry in our hearts. Taste it — taste the love."

---

### Scene 12: Abuelita's Teaching
**Setting:** Walking through the market
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita
**Action:** As they walk, Abuelita explains: everything here — flowers, skulls, candles, bread — was made by hand with intention. That's what gives it power.
**Key Visual:** Grandmother and granddaughter walking hand in hand, market life around them
**Dialogue Seed:** "Money can't buy what's in these things, mija. The hands that made them put their hearts inside."

---

### Scene 13: The Ofrenda Materials
**Setting:** Craft supply stall
**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita choosing items
**Action:** They buy papel picado (cut paper banners), photos frames, fabric for their ofrenda at home. Abuelita lets young Frances choose the colors.
**Key Visual:** Colorful craft materials, young Frances carefully selecting
**Dialogue Seed:** Abuelita: "You choose. This is your gift to them. What colors would make them happy?"

---

### Scene 14: Frances's Choice
**Setting:** Same stall
**Characters:** Young Frances deciding
**Action:** Young Frances takes her time — this is important. She picks purple and gold, remembering her abuelo loved those colors. Her first creative decision that mattered.
**Key Visual:** Young girl holding up papel picado, thoughtful and confident
**Dialogue Seed:** "Abuelo's favorite. I want him to see these colors and know we remember."

### Scene 15: Building the Ofrenda

**Characters:** Young Frances, Abuelita, extended family
**Action:** Quick montage: the family builds their ofrenda together. Flowers, candles, photos, skulls, bread — all arranged with care and love.
**Key Visual:** Beautiful ofrenda taking shape, multiple generations working
**Dialogue Seed:** (Narration) "That night, we built something together. Not for money. Not for praise. For love."

## Activity Tie-In

**Crafts That Honor**
Students create paper marigolds (tissue paper flowers) and memory cards for someone special. Discussion: What makes handmade gifts different from store-bought? Who would you make something to remember?

| Duration | Activity |
|----------|----------|
| 20 min | Make paper marigolds. Discuss: "Who would you make something for?" |
| 30 min | Marigolds + memory card for someone special. Optional sharing. |
| 45 min | Full craft station: marigolds, frames, memory cards. Create small class display. Discuss meaning vs. price. |

**Budget Tiers:**
- Tier 1 ($0-3): Tissue paper, cardstock, crayons, scissors
- Tier 2 ($8-12): Better craft supplies, frames, display board
- Tier 3 ($20+): Full ofrenda supplies, cultural books, guest speaker
